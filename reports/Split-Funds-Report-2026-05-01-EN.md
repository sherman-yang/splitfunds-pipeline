### 3.4 Long-Term View (1–3 Years and Beyond)

The most reliable long-term feature of the asset class is **the "extension" behavior of Split Funds**. Brompton and Quadravest products are nearly always extended at maturity rather than redeemed at par, which means preferred holders should treat a "$10 maturity value" as a "term-extension and coupon-reset event" rather than a guaranteed cash repayment. ## 4. Investment Methodology and Core Formulas

### 4.1 Core Formulas

```text
Class A NAV          = Unit NAV − Pfd par value
Discount / premium   = (Class A market price − Class A NAV) / Class A NAV
NAV margin of safety = (Unit NAV − distribution-suspension threshold) / threshold
Implied leverage     = Unit NAV / Class A NAV
Asset coverage       = Unit NAV / Pfd par value
YTM (approximation)  = [Annual coupon + (Par − Purchase price) / Years to maturity] / [(Par + Purchase price) / 2]
```

**Key mechanics:**
- **Death spiral.** As Unit NAV approaches the distribution-suspension threshold, Class A NAV trends toward zero and implied leverage rises non-linearly from ~2x toward infinity. At that point even modest declines in underlying assets are amplified 5–10x in the Class A price.
- **Discount mean reversion.** The annual open-end redemption + quarterly retraction features of split-corp structures prevent Class A discounts from widening indefinitely. A 5–15% discount range is the typical equilibrium; deep discounts (≥10%) are a contrarian opportunity signal.
- **YTM and the liquidity trap.** When daily preferred volume is below 5,000 shares, the last-trade price is highly susceptible to distortion; the bid/ask midpoint must be used. A large number of preferreds in this report could not produce verified bid/ask data even after multi-source checks; YTM estimates use mid-of-day-range and are accordingly degraded to **Medium** confidence.

### 4.5 Tax Considerations at a Glance

- **Canadian tax residents:** Class A distributions are a mix of eligible dividends + ROC + capital gains, and qualify for the eligible-dividend tax credit. Pfd distributions are eligible dividends (also eligible for the credit).
- **TFSA:** Tax-free, optimal for high-yield Class A names (avoids the long-term ACB-erosion bookkeeping issues from ROC).
- **RRSP:** Suitable for USD-denominated preferreds (PVS.PR.U/V) to avoid 15% U.S. withholding tax.
- **Non-registered:** ROC inside a high-yield Class A is actually advantageous (deferred tax — reduces ACB and is realized as a capital gain on disposition); USD preferreds in non-registered accounts **are subject to 15% U.S. withholding tax** — a W-8BEN must be filed.

## 5. Long-Term Total Return and Tax Disclosure

> [!WARNING]
> **This section is a mandatory disclosure and is not optional.**

Monthly and quarterly distributions on Split Funds and Split Funds ETFs may consist of five or more components — eligible dividends, capital gains dividends, return of capital (ROC), foreign income, and other income. This report deliberately does not output figures of the form "Product X 5-year/10-year annualized X.X%," for the following reasons:

1. **Price charts omit distributions.** Third-party "5-year total return" charts on Canadian split corps frequently fail to capture ROC and special-distribution reinvestment.
2. **ROC is not free money.** Headline Class A yields of 13–15% almost certainly include ROC: ACB reduction nominally defers tax, but long-term NAV is steadily withdrawn, and true economic return is overstated.
3. **Tax residency drives investability.** The eligible-dividend credit on preferreds and Class A shares applies only to Canadian tax residents.
4. **Term extensions and resets.** Nearly every Quadravest and Brompton product has been extended at least once. The "coupon reset" event on the extension date appears as a price gap on a simple price chart, but the actual economic effect is neutral-to-negative for preferred holders.

**All "current yields" in this report are computed from same-day market price + current distribution rate; all YTMs are approximated from same-day market price + par + remaining years; all "long-term return" judgments are limited to structural conclusions and avoid pseudo-precise percentage figures.**

## 10. Class A Investment Strategy (Deep Section)

### 10.1 Class A Essential Characteristics

Class A is the leveraged equity-like exposure within the Split Corp structure — entitled to the upside, exposed to the downside, and subordinate to the preferred. With Pfd par at $10 and Unit NAV of $X, Class A NAV = X − 10, and **implied leverage = X / (X − 10).** At X = $25, leverage is 1.67x; at $20, 2.00x; at $17, 2.43x; at $15, 3.00x (suspension threshold); at $12, 6.00x. **This is non-linear amplification** — as NAV approaches the threshold, leverage rises sharply, and a small decline in the underlying can produce 5–10x amplified moves in Class A price.

See § 4.3 for the implied-leverage scenario table.

### 10.2 Four Mandatory Pre-Purchase Checks for Class A

1. **NAV margin of safety (most critical):** Must be ≥ 30% (i.e., Unit NAV at least 30% above the suspension threshold). Below 25% is the danger zone.
2. **Class A market price vs. NAV discount/premium:** ≥ 5% discount provides alpha; ≥ 1% premium should be avoided.
3. **Distribution yield vs. sustainability:** Yields > 10% must contain ROC and covered-call premia; cross-check the underlying dividend yield + reasonableness of premium income; yields < 5% may signal higher-quality (growth-stock) underlyings.
4. **Maturity and time value:** ≤ 18 months remaining requires monitoring for extension events; 5+ years allows set-and-forget; 2–3 years is mainstream.

### 10.3 Three Operationally Independent Class A Total-Return Strategies

#### Strategy A: Discount + High Distribution (Income-Oriented)

**Objective:** High monthly cash flow + discount mean reversion = 15–20% annualized total return (pre-tax).

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Screen Class A: discount ≥ 5% + NAV margin of safety ≥ 30% + stable distributions over the past 12 months |
| 2 | Single-position size ≤ 10% of total portfolio; same-sector (banks/energy/REIT) ≤ 25% |
| 3 | Prefer monthly distributors (Brompton/Quadravest monthly); turn off DRIP and retain cash |
| 4 | Set reversion target: trim 50% when Class A returns to NAV parity or +1% |
| 5 | Force-trim entirely if NAV margin of safety falls below 25% |

**Suitable names:** LBS.TO (−6.56% discount + 53.40% margin of safety); FFN.TO (−11.36% discount + 26.76% margin of safety; small position); FTN.TO (−9.17% discount + 31.79% margin of safety); PDV.TO (−12.79% discount but stale NAV; verification required).

**When to choose this approach (rather than the others):** The investor explicitly seeks the dual engine of "monthly cash flow + short-to-medium-term discount reversion"; already holds an ETF core and is adding alpha; accepts long-term ROC erosion and hedges via a 12–18 month holding period.

#### Strategy B: Deep Discount + NAV Recovery (Value-Oriented)

**Objective:** Bottom-fish in sector pullbacks + valuation recovery in the underlying = 25–40% annualized total return (high risk, high reward).

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Trigger: target Class A discount ≥ 10% **and** the underlying index (XFN.TO / TSX Energy / TSX REIT) has retraced ≥ 15% from its 52-week high |
| 2 | NAV margin of safety has fallen into the 20–30% zone (dangerous but pre-suspension) |
| 3 | Three-tranche entry (add 1/3 each 5% drawdown); single-name position ≤ 5% |
| 4 | Hold 6–12 months awaiting underlying recovery; trim by half when margin of safety recovers to ≥ 40%; close entirely at ≥ 50% |
| 5 | Stop-loss entirely if NAV margin of safety falls to ≤ 5% (death-spiral region) |

**Suitable names:** No triggers in the current environment — all Class A names retain ≥ 15% margin of safety. Should XFN retrace −20% in mid-2026, FFN.TO + DFN.TO + LCS.TO would be the primary targets.

**When to choose this approach:** The investor has a 6–12 month holding period and 5–10% single-name drawdown tolerance; can distinguish between sector-level and idiosyncratic mispricing; accepts 15–30% short-term volatility on a single name in pursuit of long-term alpha.

#### Strategy C: Robust Core Allocation (Foundational)

**Objective:** Long hold + monthly income + low turnover = 8–12% annualized steady-state total return (pre-tax).

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Screen Class A: NAV margin of safety ≥ 50% + discount of 0% to −3% + long maturity (≥ 4 years) |
| 2 | Equal-weight 3–4 names + sector diversification (banks / global dividend / infrastructure / single blue-chip) |
| 3 | DRIP on for compounding (TFSA / RRSP); off in non-registered to retain cash |
| 4 | Quarterly check: trim half if Unit NAV margin of safety falls below 30%; trim entirely below 20% |
| 5 | 5–7 year horizon; do not respond to single-name discount/premium fluctuations |

**Suitable names:** Equal-weight basket of **GDV.TO (56.80% / long maturity 2031), SBC.TO (58.07%), IS.TO (102.13%), ENS.TO (99.67%).**

**When to choose this approach:** The investor's objective is a retirement-account core (TFSA / RRSP); is unwilling to make active alpha decisions; accepts 8–12% rather than 20%+ but requires NAV stability; does not make sector calls — only structural calls (margin of safety).

> [!NOTE]
> **Strategy independence self-check:** Strategy A is "active short-to-medium-term discount + high distribution"; Strategy B is "medium-term deep value, high risk"; Strategy C is "long-term low-turnover core." The three differ across all four dimensions (holding period, volatility tolerance, return objective, operational frequency); removing any one leaves the remaining two unable to cover the other type of need. ✓

### 10.4 Class A Risk Management Essentials

**Four situations to mandatorily avoid:**
1. NAV margin of safety < 20% (i.e., Unit NAV − threshold < 20%) — death-spiral activation zone.
2. Class A trading at ≥ 1% premium — alpha already exhausted.
3. Headline yields of 14%+ derived from a high ROC ratio — long-term NAV is steadily extracted.
4. AUM < CAD 50M and average daily volume < 5,000 shares — liquidity trap.

**Five metrics to monitor continuously:**

| Metric | Frequency | Source | Action threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unit NAV | Weekly (issuer) | NAV pages on bromptongroup.com / quadravest.com / middlefield.com | Trim half if margin of safety < 30% |
| Discount/premium | Daily (trading days) | TMX / Globe and Mail | Trim if premium ≥ 1% |
| Underlying index (XFN / Energy / REIT) | Daily | Yahoo Finance | Activate Strategy B at −15% from 52-week high |
| Rate environment (BoC + Prime) | Monthly | bankofcanada.ca | Reassess Class A leverage exposure on a BoC hike of ≥ 50 bp |
| Distribution status (suspension / increase / cut) | Monthly (issuer announcements) | Issuer press release | Decide on holding within 24h of any change |

**Class A Total-Return Decomposition Formula:**

```text
Class A total return = leveraged underlying dividends + discount-reversion return + NAV capital appreciation
                       − ROC erosion − covered-call premium recapture
                     ≈ 1.7x × underlying dividend yield + (discount % / holding period) + ΔNAV − 30%–40% of headline yield
```

In practice, true economic return within a 12% headline yield is roughly 6–8%; the remainder is "book distributions but NAV erosion" from ROC + covered-call premia.

## 11. Preferred Shares Investment Strategy (Deep Section)

### 11.1 Preferred Shares Essential Characteristics

The preferred is the fixed-income-like exposure within the Split Corp structure, with priority for distributions and maturity repayment. Even when Class A has been suspended, preferred holders continue to receive distributions in priority (preferreds are only at real risk if coverage falls below 100% and NAV continues to decline). **Downside-protection example:** Unit NAV of $18 vs. Pfd par of $10 = 200% coverage, meaning even a 50% decline in the underlying NAV leaves Pfd holders' $10 principal fully recoverable. Only when Unit NAV falls to $7 (asset coverage 70%) does the Pfd face a 30% book loss. **At Unit NAV of $7, Class A NAV is already −$3 — fully wiped out.** This is the fundamental difference in risk positioning between preferreds and Class A.

### 11.2 Five Mandatory Pre-Purchase Checks for Preferreds

1. **Yield-to-maturity (most critical):** Target YTM ≥ matched-maturity GIC + 150 bp, or matched-maturity 5-year Canada + 200 bp.
2. **Purchase price vs. par:** Premium > 3% requires higher yield compensation; deep discount (≥ 5%) is an opportunity signal.
3. **DBRS rating:** Pfd-3 is the floor; Pfd-2 is one notch above Pfd-3.
4. **Rate type:** Fixed vs. floating (Prime-linked) vs. hybrid; suited to different rate environments.
5. **NAV asset coverage:** < 130% caution; 130–150% monitor; ≥ 150% safe; ≥ 200% very safe.

### 11.3 Three (+1) Operationally Independent Preferred Strategies

#### Strategy A: Hold-to-Maturity for Income (Core Strategy)

**Objective:** Lock in high YTM and recover par at maturity = 5–6% annualized after-tax return.

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Screen preferreds: YTM ≥ 5% + rating ≥ Pfd-3 + asset coverage ≥ 150% + 2–5 year tenor |
| 2 | Prefer fixed-rate (avoids reinvestment risk) |
| 3 | Diversify across 3–5 names; single-position ≤ 15% of fixed-income sleeve |
| 4 | Hold to maturity or extension event; reassess reset terms upon extension |
| 5 | Hold in TFSA / non-registered (eligible-dividend credit); avoid RRSP (wastes the credit) |

**Suitable names:** **NPS.PR.A** (5.80% YTM); **FFN.PR.A** (5.62% YTM); **FTN.PR.A** (5.45% YTM + 6% floor); **IS.PR.A** (5.50% YTM + 303% coverage).

**When to choose this approach:** With the BoC cycle near its trough (2.25%), locking in fixed-preferred YTMs is relatively optimal; the investor has low risk tolerance, accepts 5–6% rather than 20%+, and avoids active management.

#### Strategy B: Floating-Rate Hedge (Hike Environment)

**Objective:** In a BoC-hike or rates-stay-high scenario, capture limited rate-upside exposure + 5% floor protection = 5–7% annualized floating return.

| Step | Action |

**When to choose this approach:** The investor expects a BoC hiking cycle (although H1 2026 consensus is for cuts); hedging the price-decline risk on fixed preferreds in a hike environment; accepts the 8% coupon cap.

## 12. Advanced Allocation Strategies

### 12.1 Split Fund 60/40 vs. Traditional 60/40

**Critical insight: with Class A implied leverage of ~1.7–2.3x, "60% Class A + 40% Pfd" ≠ traditional 60% equities + 40% bonds.**

**True equity-exposure conversion:**

| Allocation | Class A actual equity exposure | Pfd bond-like exposure | Total | Equivalent traditional allocation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 60% Class A (1.75x) + 40% Pfd | 60% × 1.75 = **105%** | 40% | 145% | ≈ 100% equity + 45% bond-like = leveraged 145/100 |
| 50% Class A (1.75x) + 50% Pfd | 50% × 1.75 = **87.5%** | 50% | 137.5% | ≈ 88% equity + 50% bond-like |
| 40% Class A (1.75x) + 60% Pfd | 40% × 1.75 = **70%** | 60% | 130% | ≈ 70% equity + 60% bond-like (enhanced traditional 70/30) |
| 30% Class A (1.75x) + 70% Pfd | 30% × 1.75 = **52.5%** | 70% | 122.5% | ≈ 50% equity + 70% bond-like |

**Comparison table:**

| Dimension | Traditional 60/40 (60% TSX + 40% bonds) | Split Fund 60/40 (60% Class A + 40% Pfd) |
|---|---|---|
| Cash flow | TSX yield ~3% + bond yield 3.2% = ~3.1% | Class A yield ~10–14% + Pfd yield ~6% = **~9–12%** |
| Bull market | 60% × equity gain | 60% × 1.75 × equity gain = **+75% excess** |
| Bear market | 60% × equity loss (no amplification) | 60% × 1.75 × loss + death-spiral risk |
| Rebalancing dividend | Standard 5–10% bands | Discount/premium fluctuations provide an **additional alpha source** |
| Tax efficiency | TSX dividends eligible + bond interest ordinary | Class A eligible dividends + Pfd eligible dividends (full tax-efficiency optimization) |
| Long-term total return | ~5–7% annualized (after-tax) | ~7–10% annualized (after-tax, assuming no long-term NAV erosion) |

**Recommended weights (by risk tolerance):**
- **Conservative (retirement / income):** 30% Class A + 70% Pfd → ~52% effective equity exposure.
- **Balanced:** 40% Class A + 60% Pfd → ~70% effective equity exposure.
- **Aggressive:** 50% Class A + 50% Pfd → ~88% effective equity exposure.
- **Not recommended ≥ 60% Class A:** cumulative leverage too high; bear-market death-spiral risk.

**Tactical execution:**

1. **Stagger entry (avoid one-shot timing risk):** Build the offensive sleeve over 6 months in equal monthly tranches; the defensive sleeve can be built in one go.
2. **5% band rebalancing:** Rebalance when either sleeve drifts ≥ 5% from target; do not rebalance more frequently (avoid unnecessary tax friction).
3. **Turn off DRIP, retain cash:** In non-registered accounts, DRIP introduces ROC-tracking complexity; retain cash and reinvest periodically.
4. **Radar triggers:** Do not add to a single offensive name on a −20% drawdown (wait for −30%, then add 1/3); trim defensive split-pref allocations by half when premium > 1%.

**Recommended ranges:** 40–50% offense / 50–60% defense. **More aggressive (under 35 + stable income) can go to 50/50**; more conservative (over 55) should reduce to 30/70.

> [!CAUTION]
> **Leveraged ETF risk warning (mandatory reading):**
> 1. **Volatility decay:** Daily-resetting 3x/4x ETFs underperform 3× the underlying return in volatile sideways markets. Example: with the S&P 500 flat (frequent ±5% chop), UPRO can lose 5–15% over a year.
> 2. **Catastrophic drawdowns:** Backtests of 2000–2002 and 2008–2009 imply UPRO-equivalent drawdowns of −80% to −95%, requiring 5x+ rallies to break even. Even within a 10-year bull market, UPRO has shown a −79% interim drawdown.
> 3. **Blow-up risk:** While leveraged ETFs do not technically go to zero (reset mechanism), holders cannot wait for a recovery once they approach zero (already 95%+ losses).
> 4. **Suitable only for:** investors with very high risk tolerance + strict execution discipline + long holding periods (5+ years) + single-position size ≤ 5% of total assets + entries when market volatility is below historical averages.
> 5. **Cannot serve as general advice:** Most retail investors (especially in retirement) should remain in a traditional 60/40 or a Split Fund 30–50% offensive sleeve and avoid 3x ETFs.
